South Carolina Personal Injury Law — What You Need to Know

Statute of Limitations

Under S.C. Code § 15-3-530, personal injury lawsuits in South Carolina must be filed within three years of the date of injury. For medical malpractice cases, a three-year statute applies from the date of the act or omission, with a six-year statute of repose under S.C. Code § 15-3-545. Missing the filing deadline permanently bars your claim, so early consultation with an attorney is critical.

Comparative Fault Rule

South Carolina applies a modified comparative fault rule. You may recover damages if you bear less than 51% of the fault for your injury. If your share reaches 51% or higher, you are completely barred from compensation. When your fault is 50% or below, your damages are reduced proportionally. Defense attorneys in Richland County routinely argue comparative fault to deflect liability — our team anticipates and counters these strategies with detailed evidence and expert testimony.

What to Do After an Injury in Columbia

  1. Get medical treatment immediately. Prisma Health Richland operates the region's busiest emergency department and Level I trauma center. Lexington Medical Center and Providence Health also serve the metro area.
  2. File a formal report. For traffic accidents, contact the Columbia Police Department or Richland County Sheriff's Office. Workplace injuries should be reported to your employer in compliance with South Carolina workers' compensation law.
  3. Document the scene. Photograph the accident location, your injuries, vehicle damage, and any unsafe conditions. Note the date, time, weather, and contact information for witnesses.
  4. Do not give a recorded statement to the at-fault party's insurance company until you have spoken with an attorney.

Types of Compensation Available Under South Carolina Law

Columbia injury victims may pursue economic damages — including medical expenses (past and future), lost wages, diminished earning capacity, and out-of-pocket costs — as well as non-economic damages for pain and suffering, emotional distress, loss of enjoyment of life, and scarring or disfigurement. Where the defendant's conduct was reckless, willful, or wanton, South Carolina law permits punitive damages under S.C. Code § 15-32-520, typically capped at the greater of three times compensatory damages or $500,000.
