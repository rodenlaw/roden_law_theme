Charleston's vibrant retail, restaurant, and hospitality districts — concentrated along King Street, Market Street, and East Bay Street — attract millions of visitors annually. South Carolina Personal Injury Law — What You Need to Know

Statute of Limitations

Under S.C. Code § 15-3-530, you have three years from the date of injury to file a personal injury lawsuit in South Carolina. While three years may seem like ample time, evidence deteriorates, witnesses become harder to locate, and insurance companies benefit from delay. We recommend consulting an attorney as soon as possible after an injury to protect your claim.

Comparative Fault Rule

South Carolina follows a modified comparative fault system. You can recover damages only if your share of fault is less than 51%. If you are found 51% or more at fault, you are barred from recovery. If your fault is 50% or below, your award is reduced by your percentage of responsibility. Defense attorneys and insurance adjusters in Charleston aggressively argue comparative fault to minimize payouts — our team pushes back with facts and evidence.

What to Do After an Injury in Charleston

  1. Seek immediate medical care. MUSC Health (Medical University of South Carolina) is Charleston's Level I trauma center. Roper St. Francis Healthcare also operates emergency departments across the metro area.
  2. File a report. Report traffic accidents to the Charleston Police Department or Charleston County Sheriff's Office. Workplace injuries should be reported to your employer per South Carolina workers' compensation requirements.
  3. Preserve evidence. Take photographs of the accident scene, your injuries, property damage, and any hazardous conditions. Collect names and phone numbers of witnesses.
  4. Do not provide recorded statements to the at-fault party's insurance carrier before you have legal representation.

Types of Compensation Available Under South Carolina Law

South Carolina personal injury plaintiffs may recover economic damages (medical bills, lost wages, future lost earnings, rehabilitation, and related out-of-pocket expenses) and non-economic damages (pain and suffering, mental anguish, loss of enjoyment of life, and loss of consortium). In cases involving reckless, willful, or wanton conduct, South Carolina law allows punitive damages under S.C. Code § 15-32-520, generally capped at three times compensatory damages or $500,000, whichever is greater.
